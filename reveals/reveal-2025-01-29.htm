"It is not from the benevolence of the butcher, the brewer, or the baker that we expect our dinner, but from their regard to their own self-interest."

- Adam Smith (1759)

Adam Smith


The quote originates from Adam Smith's The Wealth of Nations (1776), a foundational economic text discussing market dynamics and the role of self-interest in creaing societal welfare. Smith explains that socially beneficial behaviors are motivated by self-interest rather than altruism and provides the basis of laissez-faire capitalism. This idea aligns with his concept of the "invisible hand”.


Some scholars identify an "Adam Smith Problem," questioning whether his Wealth of Nations emphasizes self-interest while The Theory of Moral Sentiments centers on human sympathy and morality. Interpreted through sympathy, market exchanges reflect mutual anticipation of needs, requiring individuals to consider others’ desires. Money facilitates these exchanges but may obscure their inherently relational nature.


Additionally, critics of the simplified “self-interest” explanation argue that Smith’s focus extends beyond self-interest to the persuasive, social nature of economic interactions, where reason and communication foster mutual benefit. This interpretation highlights Smith’s nuanced understanding of economics as deeply tied to human interaction and cooperation.
