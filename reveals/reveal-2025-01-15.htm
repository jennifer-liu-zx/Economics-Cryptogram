"Everything is worth what its purchaser will pay for it."

- Publilius Syrus, 1st century BC

Publilius Syrus


Publilius Syrus was a performer, and his writings live on in is a collection of Sententiae, a series of moral maxims in iambic and trochaic verse.


The quote itself calls into question (and answers) how value should be defined. The question has lingered in the minds of economists for centuries, from Adam Smith's Labour Theory of Value to the Sraffian Theory of Value. Today, the commonly accepted theory of value is the Marginal Theory of Value, the foundation of neoclassical economics which defines the value of a good by its marginal utility. Marginalism was formally proposed in the 1870s, nearly 2000 years after Syrus uttered the quote reflecting this exact sentiment.


However, the theory is not without its flaws. Economists who urge the field to take a closer look at the concept of value point out the failure of marginalism to isolate value from speculation and how imperfections in the market which should not actually affect the value of a good candistort its price. Alternate theories of value in which the value of a good, to some degree, is innate, include the Sraffian Theory of Value, which algebraically derives value from the inputs of a good.
