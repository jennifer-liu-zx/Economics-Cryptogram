"Everything is worth what its purchaser will pay for it."

- Publilius Syrus, 1st century BC

Publilius Syrus


This quote originates from Publilius Syrus’s Sententiae (The Moral Sayings of Publius Syrus), published in the 1st century BC. Syrus, a Latin mime writer and freed slave, is known for his widely cited wisdom and moral guidance. This maxim reflects the nature of value, suggesting that worth is determined subjectively, based on individual perception and willingness to pay.


In fact, the problem of how to define value has lingered in the minds of economists for centuries, from Adam Smith's Labour Theory of Value to the Sraffian Theory of Value. Today, the commonly accepted theory of value is the Marginal Theory of Value, the foundation of neoclassical economics which defines the value of a good by its marginal utility. Marginalism was formally proposed in the 1870s, nearly 2000 years after Syrus uttered the quote reflecting this exact sentiment.


However, many argue that Marginalism neglects intrinsic values like ethical, cultural, or emotional worth, and allows speculation and market imperfections, which distort the price of a good, to determine its value. Alternate theories of value in which the value of a good is, to some degree, innate, include the Sraffian Theory of Value, which algebraically derives value from the inputs of a good.
